To see that \(\mathcal{C}\) is a basis, note that it is linearly independent by Proposition 2.5.4. Indeed, the reduced row echelon form of the matrix with only these columns is the same as the submatrix of \(A^\prime\) with only these columns. In particular, it has no free columns and the cited proposition implies that \(\mathcal{C}\) is linearly independent. On the other hand, adding any other column of \(A\) to \(\mathcal{C}\) fails the test in Proposition 2.5.4 for the same reason. But that means that any other column can be written as a linear combination of the columns in \(\mathcal{C}\) (why?). Thus \(\mathcal{C}\) spans the column space and is linearly independent. By Proposition 2.5.2, \(\mathcal{C}\) is a basis for the column space.
