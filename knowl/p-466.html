To see the first equation, we note that Lemma 3.2.9 has already given us a unique decomposition \(\mb{v} = \mb{u} + \mb{u}^\prime\text{.}\) Given any vector \(\mb{\tilde{u}} \in U\) we observe that the square of the distance from \(\mb{v}\) to \(\mb{\tilde{u}}\) is
\begin{align*} \| \mb{v} - \mb{\tilde{u}} \|^2 \amp = \|\mb{u} + \mb{u}^\prime -\mb{\tilde{u}} \|^2, \\ \amp = \| ( \mb{u} - \mb{\tilde{u}} ) + \mb{u}^\prime \|^2, \\ \amp = \| \mb{u} - \mb{\tilde{u}} \|^2 + \| \mb{u}^\prime \|^2 . \end{align*}
Here we used the fact that \(\left\langle \mb{u} - \mb{\tilde{u}},\mb{u}^\prime \right\rangle = 0\) because both \(\mb{u}\) and \(\mb{\tilde{u}}\) are in \(U\) while \(\mb{u}^\prime\) is in \(U^\perp\text{.}\) Clearly, if \(\mb{u} \ne \mb{\tilde{u}}\) then this distance squared is greater than \(\|\mb{u}^\prime \|^2\text{.}\) Thus \(\mb{u}\) minimizes the distance from \(U\) to \(\mb{v}\) and is the projection. Examining the proof of Lemma 3.2.9 we see that \(\mb{u}\) is given explicitly as the linear combination in equation (3.2.3).
